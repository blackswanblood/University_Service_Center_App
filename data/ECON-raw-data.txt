	<dt><a name="390"></a>ECON 390 (3)  <b>Introduction to Economic Research</b></dt>
	<dd>Selected topics in economic research.  Application of research methods to economic data.  Credit will be granted for only one of ECON 390, ECON 490, ECON 495, or ECON 499. <em>This course is not eligible for Credit/D/Fail grading.</em> <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 101, ECON 310 and one of ECON 102, ECON 311 and one of MATH 100, MATH 102, MATH 104, MATH 110, MATH 120, MATH 180, MATH 184. MATH 104, 110 or 184 recommended.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="398"></a>ECON 398 (3)  <b>Introduction to Applied Economics</b></dt>
	<dd>Theory and practice of the analytical application of economics. Causality, empirical analysis, and the application of theoretical models through engagement in applied work. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> Either (a) one of ECON 101, ECON 310 and one of ECON 102, ECON 311 and one of STAT 200, STAT 241, STAT 251, COMM 291, FRST 231;  or (b) one of ECON 226, ECON 325, ECON 326, ECON 327. Not recommended for students who have completed any of ECON 390, ECON 490, ECON 494, ECON 499.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="406"></a>ECON 406 (3)  <b>Topics in Microeconomics</b></dt>
	<dd>Selected topics in advanced microeconomic analysis. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315 and one of ECON 303, ECON 306, ECON 316.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="407"></a>ECON 407 (3)  <b>Topics in Macroeconomics</b></dt>
	<dd>Selected topics in advanced macroeconomic analysis. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304 and one of ECON 302, ECON 305 and one of ECON 303, ECON 306.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="417"></a>ECON 417 (3)  <b>Welfare Economics</b></dt>
	<dd>The criteria for evaluating economic performance with special reference to the problems of justice in the distribution of income and economic efficiency. Topics include social evaluation functions, pareto-optimality, compensation criteria, and consistency of collective decision making. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="420"></a>ECON 420 (3)  <b>Optimization and Economic Theory</b></dt>
	<dd>An introduction to static and dynamic optimization methods with economic applications. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> MATH 200 and one of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315 and one of ECON 303, ECON 306, ECON 316. Permission of the instructor is also acceptable.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="421"></a>ECON 421 (3)  <b>Introduction to Game Theory and Applications</b></dt>
	<dd>Principles of rational behaviour in strategic situations and various notions of equilibrium useful in predicting outcomes. Applications from economics, business, politics, law and biology. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="422"></a>ECON 422 (3)  <b>Mathematics for Economists</b></dt>
	<dd>Provides the required preparation in mathematics for the study of graduate economic theory. Solving systems of simultaneous equations; unconstrained and constrained maxima; elementary theory of difference and differential equations. Restricted to students taking graduate economic theory courses. <br > 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="425"></a>ECON 425 (3)  <b>Introduction to Econometrics</b></dt>
	<dd>Theoretical and applied issues in statistics and econometrics. Statistical distributions, sampling theory, maximum likelihood methods of estimation and hypothesis testing, generalized least squares, measurement errors, non-normal errors, systems of equations, discrete-choice models, outliers, regression diagnostics, and model selection. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 325, ECON 327 and one of ECON 326, ECON 328.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="426"></a>ECON 426 (3)  <b>Econometric Analysis</b></dt>
	<dd>Further topics in econometrics including such areas as nonlinear estimation, distributed lag models, time-series analysis, time-varying parameters, multivariate analysis, simulation and forecasting models, Monte Carlo experiments, duration models, large econometric models, Bayesian statistics, asymptotic theory, and ergodic theory. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> ECON 425.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="436"></a>ECON 436 (3)  <b>Historical Background to Contemporary Issues in Canadian Economy</b></dt>
	<dd>Demographic change, immigration, unemployment and labour markets, the inter-provincial transfer of resources, industrial and trade policy. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 304, ECON 301, ECON 315 and one of ECON 302, ECON 305, ECON 309.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="441"></a>ECON 441 (3)  <b>The Process of Economic Development</b></dt>
	<dd>Industrialization of an agrarian economy; how the West grew rich; history of Japanese development; technical progress and growth; evolution of the patterns of income distribution; role of international trade in development; environment and development. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="442"></a>ECON 442 (3)  <b>Issues in Economic Development</b></dt>
	<dd>Divergence in the world economy, poverty, consequences of initial inequality, institutions, the impact of history, recent developmental experience--some case studies, labour and credit markets, the trickle down process. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="444"></a>ECON 444 (3)  <b>The Contemporary Japanese Economy</b></dt>
	<dd>An economic analysis of selected issues in contemporary Japan. The postwar growth record, economic management and planning, industrial policy, labour market and industrial relations, foreign trade and investment, rapid industrialization and its consequences, external economic relations. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="447"></a>ECON 447 (3)  <b>Monetary Theory</b></dt>
	<dd>Theoretical analysis of economies that use money; the emergence of money; the roles of money in the economy; models of money demand; the optimal quantity of money seignorage and inflation; monetary policy and macroeconomic stability; monetary policy in an open economy. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315 and one of ECON 302, ECON 305, ECON 309.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="450"></a>ECON 450 (3)  <b>Economics of Taxation</b></dt>
	<dd>The economic analysis of taxation. Equity and efficiency; optimal taxation theory; partial and general equilibrium analysis of incidence; analysis of taxes such as the personal and corporate income taxes, sales and excise taxes, payroll taxes and property tax. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="451"></a>ECON 451 (3)  <b>Economics of Public Expenditures</b></dt>
	<dd>The role of government in the economy; efficiency and economic justice. Theory of public goods; applications to topics such as education, medical care and social services. Pricing and investment rules for public enterprises. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="455"></a>ECON 455 (3)  <b>International Trade</b></dt>
	<dd>International trade theory and policy in general equilibrium; relative costs, factor proportions, imperfect competition and the pattern of trade; efficiency and distribution. Credit granted for only one of ECON355 and ECON455. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="456"></a>ECON 456 (3)  <b>International Macroeconomics and Finance</b></dt>
	<dd>Balance of payments; market for foreign exchange; mechanism for adjusting the balance of payments; internal vs. external stability; current problems and issues. Credit will be granted for only one of ECON 456, COEC 377, or COMM 377. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 302, ECON 305, ECON 309.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="457"></a>ECON 457 (3)  <b>Seminar in International Economic Relations</b></dt>
	<dd>Selected topics focusing upon various issues arising in international economic relations. Open only to fourth-year students in the Major program in International Relations. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> All of ECON 101, ECON 102.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="460"></a>ECON 460 (3)  <b>Economics of Labour Markets</b></dt>

Credit may not be obtained for both ECON 360 and 460. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315 and one of ECON 302, ECON 305, ECON 309.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="461"></a>ECON 461 (3)  <b>Economics of Trade Unions</b></dt>
	<dd>The microeconomic and macroeconomic effects of unions on wages, prices and employment. Industrial disputes and their resolution. Credit may not be obtained for both ECON 361 and 461. <br > <em>Prerequisite:</em> One of ECON 301, ECON 304, ECON 315 and one of ECON 302, ECON 305, ECON 309.<br> 
	</dd>

	<dt><a name="465"></a>ECON 465 (3)  <b>Market Structure</b></dt>

	</dd>

	<dt><a name="466"></a>ECON 466 (3)  <b>The Economics of Government Regulation of Business</b></dt>

	</dd>

	<dt><a name="468"></a>ECON 468 (3/6) d <b>Economics of Intellectual Property: Patents, Drugs, and Rock 'n Roll</b></dt>

	</dd>
